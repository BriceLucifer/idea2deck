# Narrative Patterns

Use these patterns only as adaptable starting points. Select, reorder, merge, or omit sections according to the audience, evidence, setting, and desired outcome.

## Investor pitch

Opening thesis, problem, insight, solution, product, market, evidence, business model, advantage, plan, team, ask.

Emphasize why now, credible traction, scale, defensibility, and the decision requested. Do not invent market sizes, traction, forecasts, or customer proof.

## Product launch

Audience tension, new possibility, product promise, experience, key capabilities, proof, use cases, rollout, call to action.

Lead with user value rather than a feature inventory.

## Sales proposal

Client context, current cost or risk, desired outcome, proposed approach, solution fit, evidence, delivery plan, commercial framing, next step.

Keep the client's world more prominent than the seller's company history.

## Executive briefing

Decision headline, current state, material findings, implications, options, recommendation, risks, actions.

Make each title carry a conclusion and keep supporting detail subordinate.

## Research or analytical report

Question, method and scope, major findings, evidence, interpretation, limitations, implications, recommendation.

Separate observed evidence from interpretation and preserve relevant caveats.

## Training deck

Learning outcome, context, core model, worked example, guided practice, pitfalls, recap, action or assessment.

Use progressive disclosure and avoid turning reference documentation into projected paragraphs.

## Conference talk

Hook, tension, perspective, journey or evidence, turning point, practical implications, memorable close.

Optimize for live pacing and speaker notes rather than document-like completeness.

## Portfolio or case study

Context, challenge, role, constraints, approach, key decisions, execution, result, reflection.

Show decision quality and evidence, not only polished outcomes.

## Selection guidance

When no pattern fits cleanly, derive a custom arc:

1. What should the audience understand, believe, feel, or do?
2. What do they already know or resist?
3. What evidence changes their view?
4. What order minimizes cognitive load?
5. What single conclusion should remain after the presentation?
